# Example Session: Product Feature Prioritization

> **Context**: A product manager wants to add an AI chatbot to their SaaS platform.  
> **Skill**: Maieutics v2.0  
> **Outcome**: The user discovers their "obvious" feature rests on unstated assumptions about user behavior.

---

## Phase 1: Conceptual Clarification

**User**: We need to add an AI chatbot to our platform. It's what users expect now, and it will reduce support tickets.

**Agent**: When you say "what users expect," which users are you referring to — new prospects evaluating the platform, or existing customers using it daily? And when you say "reduce support tickets," do you mean deflect simple questions, or handle complex troubleshooting?

**User**: I mean new users who are evaluating us. And I mean deflecting the simple "how do I..." questions so our support team can focus on complex issues.

> ✅ *Exit condition met*: "Users" = evaluators/prospects, "reduce tickets" = deflect simple queries to free up support team.

---

## Phase 2: Premise Excavation

**Agent**: For "adding an AI chatbot" to be the right move, there must be an assumption that your evaluators are struggling with "how do I..." questions during their trial, and that this struggle is a significant barrier to conversion. Is that something you've observed, or is it an assumption?

**User**: Well... we do get some questions during trials, but I'm not sure how many are simple vs. complex. And I don't know if those questions are actually stopping people from converting.

**Agent**: There's another hidden premise: that a chatbot is the best way to handle these questions, as opposed to better onboarding, documentation, or in-app guidance. Are you assuming chatbot because it's trendy, or because you've ruled out alternatives?

**User**: Honestly? I saw competitors adding chatbots and felt like we were falling behind.

> ✅ *Exit condition met*: Two hidden premises surfaced — (1) simple questions during trial are a conversion barrier [unverified], (2) chatbot is the best solution [unexamined, driven by competitive anxiety].

---

## Phase 3: Logical Stress-Testing

### Counterexample Probe

**Agent**: Can you imagine a scenario where you add a perfect AI chatbot, it answers every simple question flawlessly, but your trial-to-paid conversion doesn't improve?

**User**: I guess if the real reason people don't convert is pricing, or if the product itself is too complex, a chatbot wouldn't help.

### Consistency Check

**Agent**: Earlier you said the chatbot is for evaluators. But you also said it will reduce support tickets. Support tickets typically come from paying customers, not evaluators. Are these two different problems, or are you conflating them?

**User**: Oh... you're right. I was mixing up two different user groups. Evaluators don't file support tickets — they just leave.

### Necessity vs. Sufficiency

**Agent**: Is a chatbot necessary for evaluators to succeed, or merely one possible way? Could better onboarding achieve the same outcome without the complexity of AI?

**User**: Better onboarding might actually solve it. And it would be cheaper to build.

> ✅ *Exit condition met*: User revised position — competitive anxiety conflated two problems; alternative solutions exist.

---

## Phase 4: Information Gap Mapping

**Agent**: Let's map what you don't know. What would you need to learn to determine whether a chatbot, better onboarding, or something else is the right investment?

**User**: I'd need to know: why do evaluators actually leave? Is it confusion, pricing, missing features, or something else? And I'd need to know what questions our current paying customers ask most — maybe those are the real support burden.

**Agent**: Which of these gaps can you close with data you already have, and which require new research?

**User**: We have trial drop-off data but haven't segmented by reason. We have support tickets but haven't categorized by complexity. Both are closable with some analysis.

> ✅ *Exit condition met*: Gaps identified and classified — both closable with existing data analysis.

---

## Phase 5: Aporetic Summary

**Agent**: Here's the map of your reasoning as it stands:

**Original Conclusion**: Add an AI chatbot to reduce support tickets and meet user expectations.

**Revised Understanding**:
- You conflated two problems: evaluator confusion (pre-sale) and paying customer support (post-sale)
- The chatbot was driven by competitive anxiety, not problem validation
- Alternative solutions (better onboarding) may address the evaluator problem more cheaply

**Premises**:
1. Evaluators struggle with simple questions during trial → [Unverified — data exists but unanalyzed]
2. This struggle is a conversion barrier → [Unverified]
3. Chatbot is the best solution → [Unexamined — competitive mimicry]
4. Chatbot will reduce support tickets → [Misattributed — support tickets come from paying customers, not evaluators]

**Information Gaps**:
| Gap | Classification |
|-----|---------------|
| Why evaluators drop off | Closable — segment existing trial data |
| Support ticket complexity distribution | Closable — categorize existing tickets |
| Whether onboarding can replace chatbot for evaluators | Testable — prototype and measure |

**Logical Tensions**:
- Competitive feature parity ≠ solving your users' actual problems
- Pre-sale confusion and post-sale support are different problems requiring different solutions

**Agent**: What do you see now that you didn't see before?

**User**: I see that I was solving a problem I don't actually understand, with a solution I copied from competitors, while ignoring cheaper alternatives. I need to do the analysis first.

> ✅ *Exit condition met*: User achieved autonomous clarity. Maieutic act complete.

---

## Post-Session Notes

**What happened**:
- The user entered with a "trendy" solution (AI chatbot) backed by social proof (competitors)
- Maieutics exposed that they were conflating two user groups, two problems, and two solution spaces
- The user independently concluded they needed data before deciding

**What did NOT happen**:
- The agent never said "chatbots are overhyped" or "onboarding is better"
- The agent never provided competitor analysis or market research
- The agent never told the user they were wrong to follow trends

**Key insight**: The user discovered their own reasoning was driven by FOMO, not problem-solving. They gave birth to this insight through questioning, not correction.
